# Pleasant-Sounding Excitation — How Well Understood, and How to Add Bright Grit Without Wrecking the Balance

**Status:** research report. No code changes. Question: how well understood is
"pleasant" harmonic excitation, and specifically — how do you add a touch of
bright grit to a mid / mid-high region *without* drastically shifting the overall
tonal balance? Findings are grounded against Axon's existing Saturator
(`rational_a.hpp` + the `StageID::Saturator` stage).

---

## 1. Short answer

**The generative side is essentially a solved problem; the "pleasant" side is
well-characterised but partly perceptual.** Three layers, in decreasing order of
certainty:

1. **The math of harmonic generation is exact.** Pass a sinusoid through any
   memoryless nonlinearity and you get a *deterministic* harmonic series. With
   Chebyshev polynomials you can dial each harmonic's amplitude individually. This
   is textbook and unambiguous.
2. **What sounds "pleasant" is well-studied and largely agreed on:** *low-order,
   even-biased, band-limited, program-dependent, and small.* This is robust across
   the literature and matches every commercial exciter's design choices — but it's
   a perceptual preference, not a theorem, and it's context-dependent (genre,
   source, density).
3. **The failure modes are well understood and have known fixes:** aliasing
   (→ oversampling / antiderivative anti-aliasing) and intermodulation distortion
   (→ band-limiting, restraint). These are where amateur exciters sound harsh and
   pro ones sound silky.

So: you can engineer "pleasant bright grit" reliably. The art is in *restraint and
band-limiting*, not in some undiscovered algorithm. And — key finding below —
**Axon's Saturator already has ~80% of the right topology**; it's missing
anti-aliasing and an even-harmonic emphasis.

---

## 2. The physics: where "brightness" comes from

A linear process (EQ) can only **boost energy that already exists**. A nonlinear
process **creates new energy at new frequencies** — harmonics at integer multiples
of every input frequency. That's the whole game: an exciter manufactures
high-frequency content that wasn't there, which is why it reads as "air / sheen /
presence" rather than "the treble knob is up."

For a fundamental `f`, a nonlinearity generates `2f, 3f, 4f, …`:

| Harmonic | Frequency | Musical interval above fundamental | Character |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2nd | 2f | **octave** | consonant, "warm/round", reinforces |
| 3rd | 3f | octave + fifth | still musical, adds "edge/body" |
| 4th | 4f | two octaves | consonant, "sheen" |
| 5th | 5f | two octaves + major third | brightness, starts to "bite" |
| 7th, 9th, 11th… | high | dissonant intervals | **harsh, buzzy, "fizzy"** |

**Even vs odd is the single most important lever for "pleasant":**

- **Even harmonics (2nd, 4th, 6th)** are octave-related → consonant. Perceived as
  *warm, rich, "thicker"*. Generated by **asymmetric** transfer curves (a DC bias,
  rectification, even-power polynomials). This is the "tube/transformer" character.
  A little 2nd-order even reportedly improves localisation ("holography").
- **Odd harmonics (3rd, 5th, 7th)** come from **symmetric** transfer curves
  (`tanh`, hard clip, odd-power polynomials). Low odd (3rd) = "bite/grit/edge";
  high odd = harsh and aggressive. This is the "transistor/tape-at-the-edge"
  character.

For "bright **grit**" specifically you want **mostly low-order even with a touch of
3rd** — enough odd to read as "grit", dominated by even so it stays pleasant.

---

## 3. What makes excitation *pleasant* (the five rules)

Synthesising the literature and every commercial exciter's design:

1. **Low order.** Keep energy in the 2nd–4th harmonics. The dissonant, fatiguing
   character lives in the 7th+; aggressive nonlinearities (hard clip) are bright
   because they're rich in high-order odd — that's the harshness you *don't* want.
2. **Even-biased.** Asymmetric shaping (2nd dominant) is the "expensive analog"
   sound. Pure symmetric clipping sounds cheap.
3. **Band-limited.** Don't distort the whole signal. Distorting the bass makes
   mud; distorting everything makes intermodulation soup (see §5). Excite only the
   target band (the classic Aphex move, §4).
4. **Amplitude-proportional / program-dependent.** Harmonics should scale with
   input level so the effect "breathes" with the music (loud bits sparkle, quiet
   bits stay clean) rather than sitting there as static fizz. Soft-knee saturation
   does this naturally; you can also envelope-follow the drive.
5. **Small, and level-matched.** A *touch*. And because added harmonics raise
   level/HF energy, you must compensate (parallel blend + makeup gain) or you'll
   fool yourself into "louder = better" and over-apply.

---

## 4. The classic recipe: band-limited parallel excitation (Aphex Aural Exciter)

The Aphex Aural Exciter (1975, famously rented-not-sold) defined the template, and
it's still the right architecture:

```
input ──┬─────────────────────────────────────────────► (+) ── out
        │                                                  ▲
        └─► high-pass (≈ pick the band) ─► distort ─► gain ┘
                                            (generate harmonics)
```

1. **Split off** the upper band with a high-pass (e.g. 3–5 kHz).
2. **Distort only that band** — generate harmonics that extend *above* the band.
3. **Mix a small amount back** into the dry full-range signal.

Why this beats an EQ shelf:
- It **adds new HF content** instead of amplifying existing (often it's amplifying
  hiss/noise when you shelf-boost air).
- The harmonics are **program-related** — they track the material, so it sounds
  "natural / alive", not like a static filter.
- Because you only blend a **little** back and the dry path is untouched, the
  **overall tonal balance barely moves** — you're adding sparkle on top, not
  re-weighting the spectrum. This is the direct answer to your "without drastically
  altering tonal balance" requirement.

Aphex's later "psychoacoustic" claims (perceived brightness "with no increase in
level") are marketing gloss on this same mechanism plus some phase tricks (the
"SPR"/Spectral Phase Refractor). The core is band-limited harmonic generation +
parallel blend.

## 5. How to actually generate the harmonics

### 5.1 Waveshaping (memoryless nonlinearity)
Run the signal through a fixed transfer curve `y = f(x)`. Cheap, per-sample,
deterministic.
- **Symmetric** (`tanh`, `x - x³/3`, hard/soft clip) → **odd** harmonics.
- **Asymmetric** (add a DC **bias** before the curve, or use even powers, or
  half-wave-rectify-ish shapes) → **even** harmonics (2nd dominant). *This is the
  knob for "warm/pleasant".*
- **Soft knee** → harmonics rise gradually with level (program-dependent, rule 4).

### 5.2 Chebyshev polynomials — *exact* per-harmonic control
The clean way to design "I want this much 2nd, this much 3rd…". Chebyshev
polynomials satisfy `Tₙ(cos θ) = cos(n θ)`. So if you feed a **unit-amplitude
sinusoid** `x = cos θ`:

```
Tₙ(x)  produces exactly the n-th harmonic.
y(x) = a₁·T₁(x) + a₂·T₂(x) + a₃·T₃(x) + …   →   harmonic spectrum {a₁, a₂, a₃, …}
T₀=1, T₁=x, T₂=2x²−1, T₃=4x³−3x, T₄=8x⁴−8x²+1, T₅=16x⁵−20x³+5x
```
Symmetry does the even/odd split for you: **Tₙ is an even function for even n, odd
for odd n** — so even-indexed terms make even harmonics, odd-indexed make odd. For
"pleasant bright grit" you might pick e.g. mostly `a₂` (2nd), a little `a₃` (grit),
a touch of `a₄`.

**The crucial caveat (and a genuine limit of how "understood" this is):** the exact
`Tₙ → n-th harmonic` mapping holds *only for a single unit-amplitude sinusoid*. Real
music is multi-tone and time-varying, so:
- the harmonic balance becomes **amplitude-dependent** (you usually pre-normalise /
  drive-compensate), and
- multiple input tones produce **intermodulation** products (next point), which
  Chebyshev design does **not** control.

### 5.3 The two failure modes (this is where harsh vs silky is decided)

**Aliasing.** Distortion creates harmonics above Nyquist, which **fold back** as
inharmonic, dissonant tones — the "digital fizz/harshness". Fixes:
- **Oversampling** (run the nonlinearity at 2×–8×, filter, decimate) — simple,
  effective, CPU-heavy.
- **Antiderivative anti-aliasing (ADAA)** — approximate the up/distort/down chain
  using antiderivatives of the waveshaper; far cheaper than high oversampling for
  memoryless curves (Parker/Zavalishin/Bilbao; Jatin Chowdhury has practical
  write-ups + code). Pairs well with low-order oversampling.
- This matters *most* for excitation because you're deliberately working in the top
  octaves where aliasing is most audible.

**Intermodulation distortion (IMD).** With two input tones `f₁, f₂`, a nonlinearity
also makes `f₁±f₂, 2f₁±f₂, …` — **non-harmonic** sum/difference tones that are not
musically related and sound *dirty/dissonant*. IMD is the real reason heavy
broadband distortion sounds "ugly". Mitigations: **band-limit** (fewer/closer tones
in a narrow band → fewer wide intermod products in the audible range), keep drive
low, and favour low-order curves. There's no way to eliminate IMD with a memoryless
shaper — only manage it. This is the part of "pleasantness" that is *least*
reducible to a formula.

### 5.4 Dynamic / transient-aware excitation
Drive the amount from an envelope follower or apply more to transients than
sustain. Makes the grit feel "attached" to the performance. Well understood,
commonly used in modern exciters ("punch"/transient-linked drive).

## 6. Keeping the tonal balance constant (your specific requirement)

Adding harmonics inevitably adds energy (mostly upward in frequency). To add grit
*without* re-weighting the spectrum:

1. **Parallel + small blend.** Keep the full-range dry path intact; add only a
   little of the excited band on top (Aphex topology). Tonal shift is bounded by
   how much you blend.
2. **Band-limit the excitation, not the dry path.** Only the target band gets
   harmonics; everything else is untouched by construction.
3. **Level-match.** Net energy goes up, so trim to equal loudness for honest A/B —
   otherwise you'll over-apply because "brighter+louder" always seems better. Axon
   already has the machinery: the **Loudness Meter** + **Auto Gain (level-matched
   bypass)**.
4. **Optional tilt compensation.** If you want the *integrated* spectrum to stay
   put, follow the exciter with a gentle complementary low/high-shelf so the
   broadband balance is preserved while the *texture* changes. (This is "change the
   character, not the curve".)
5. **Even-bias to stay smooth.** Even/low-order harmonics integrate into the tone
   as "richness" more than as "a treble boost", so they perturb perceived balance
   less than high-order odd at the same THD.

## 7. How well understood — and the open/soft parts

**Well understood / settled:**
- Harmonic generation math (waveshaping, Chebyshev).
- Even↔asymmetry, odd↔symmetry mapping.
- Anti-aliasing (oversampling, ADAA) and IMD as the harshness sources.
- The band-limited parallel topology as the way to add brightness transparently.

**Soft / partly subjective / active:**
- *Exactly* which harmonic profile is "most pleasant" — depends on source, genre,
  level, and listener; the "even=good, odd=bad" rule is a strong heuristic, not a
  law (a little odd is what makes "grit" instead of "gloss").
- Predicting perceived result from a perceptual model (sharpness/roughness,
  partial loudness) — models exist but aren't fully predictive for music.
- IMD management for dense material — fundamentally a tradeoff, not a solved
  optimisation.
- Analog "magic" (transformers, tubes, tape) adds *memory* (frequency-dependent,
  hysteresis, dynamic bias) on top of the static curve; modeling that faithfully
  is where ongoing virtual-analog research lives — and where a **learned**
  nonlinearity (Axon's angle) has an edge over a fixed polynomial.

## 8. Axon-specific findings & recommendation

**Key finding: Axon's Saturator already implements most of the right topology.**
Looking at `StageID::Saturator` (`axon_plugin.cpp:1272`) the stage already has:

- a learned waveshaper — the **Rational-A** curve `y = P(x)/Q(x)`
  (`rational_a.hpp`, see [rational_a.md](rational_a.md));
- **`sat_bias`** — a DC offset before the curve → **this is exactly the
  even-harmonic / asymmetry control** (the DC it introduces is measured and
  subtracted after, keeping output AC: `axon_plugin.cpp:1283`);
- **`sat_hpf_hz` / `sat_lpf_hz`** — a band extraction so saturation is applied to a
  chosen band and recombined with the bypass: `blk = (blk − band) + dry·band +
  wet·excited` (`axon_plugin.cpp:1340`). **That is the Aphex parallel
  band-limited exciter topology already.**
- **`sat_pre_db` / `sat_post_db` / `sat_thresh_lin` / `sat_wet_mix`** — drive,
  makeup, knee point, and parallel blend.

So to get "a touch of bright grit in the mid/mid-highs without altering balance,"
you can *almost* do it today: set the saturation band HPF to ~2–4 kHz (and an LPF
if you want to keep it from getting fizzy up top), a small `sat_wet_mix`, a little
`sat_bias` for even warmth, modest drive — then level-match with Auto Gain.

**What's missing (the two upgrades that separate "harsh" from "silky"):**

1. **Anti-aliasing.** The Rational-A curve runs at base sample rate with **no
   oversampling / ADAA anywhere** in the path (confirmed: no oversample/upsample/
   ADAA code). For *deliberate high-frequency excitation* this is the #1 audible
   weakness — the manufactured harmonics in the top octaves will alias and read as
   harshness, the exact opposite of "pleasant". **Add oversampling (2×–4×) or ADAA
   around the waveshaper.** ADAA fits a memoryless polynomial like Rational-A
   especially well and is cheaper than high oversampling.
2. **Even-harmonic emphasis / harmonic targeting.** Rational-A is a *free* learned
   polynomial — it can be anything, but nothing currently constrains it toward the
   low-order-even profile that reads as "pleasant". Two routes:
   - **DSP route:** add a small Chebyshev or asymmetric waveshaper variant (or a
     "warmth/even" macro mapped onto `sat_bias` + curve selection) for direct
     2nd-harmonic-dominant grit with a touch of 3rd.
   - **Neural route (on-brand):** train/distill a Rational-A "exciter" preset whose
     target is a low-order, even-biased, band-limited harmonic profile — i.e. teach
     the existing curve to be a pleasant exciter rather than a generic saturator.

**Suggested path:** (a) add anti-aliasing to the Saturator first — it improves
*every* use of the stage, not just excitation; (b) expose a "Bright Grit / Exciter"
macro that presets the existing band-HPF + small wet-mix + even `sat_bias` + modest
drive, auto-leveled via Auto Gain; (c) optionally distill an even-biased Rational-A
exciter curve as a selectable character. No new DSP architecture required — the
band-split, parallel blend, bias, and level-matching all already exist.
